Electroencephalography (\gls{eeg}) is a widely used, non-invasive technique for measuring electrical activity generated by neural populations in the brain.
Its applications span clinical diagnostics, neuroscience research, and the development of brain–computer interfaces (\gls{bci}). As \gls{eeg} systems evolve toward
higher channel counts, wearable form factors, and tighter integration with digital signal processing pipelines, the need for reliable and reproducible validation
methodologies becomes increasingly critical.

A fundamental challenge in \gls{eeg} system evaluation lies in the absence of a known ground truth when measurements are performed on human subjects. Biological
variability, uncontrollable physiological states, and environmental noise make it difficult to isolate the performance of individual system components—such
as electrodes, acquisition hardware, or signal-processing algorithms—from the underlying neural activity. As a result, controlled testing and quantitative
benchmarking of \gls{eeg} systems remain inherently limited when relying exclusively on in-vivo experiments.

Physical head phantoms provide a controlled alternative for \gls{eeg} validation. By replicating the electrical behavior of the head and enabling the injection
of predefined signals, phantoms allow repeatable experiments under well-defined conditions. However, existing \gls{eeg} phantom solutions exhibit several practical
limitations. Many rely on specialized or proprietary materials, involve complex or expensive manufacturing processes. These factors can hinder accessibility and adaptation across research contexts.

A further limitation of current approaches is the frequent separation between physical experimentation, numerical modeling, and signal-generation software. Phantom heads are often treated as isolated test objects, without an explicit linkage between injected signals, material properties, and measured surface potentials. This separation complicates quantitative interpretation and limits the systematic tuning of phantom-based experiments.

This thesis adopts a calibration-driven approach to \gls{eeg} phantom development, centered on the explicit characterization and control of a specific phantom head. The work focuses on identifying and formalizing the electrical response of this system so that its behavior can be represented in a traceable manner within a signal-generation and measurement workflow.

The complete system is structured around three main components:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Software platform}: A dedicated application enables the definition of target \gls{eeg}-like signals and manages the corresponding signal
    routing to the internal electrode array. The software also supports visualization of system states and comparison between generated and measured
    signals, forming the operational interface between model outputs and hardware control.
    \item \textbf{Hardware specification and \gls{cad} model}: A parametric \gls{cad} model of the phantom head is developed to define the system geometry, electrode placement, and internal routing required for controlled signal generation and measurement. The design establishes a concrete physical reference for the system, with explicitly defined geometric and material parameters that serve as the basis for numerical characterization and future fabrication.
    \item \textbf{Numerical modeling and signal mapping pipeline}: A finite element method (\gls{fem}) model of the phantom head is implemented to characterize its electrical response under controlled excitation, based on the defined geometry and material properties. The resulting potential distributions are processed to extract a transfer mapping between internal electrode actuation and external voltage measurements. This mapping is formatted as control parameters directly usable by the software platform, enabling closed-loop signal generation based on the identified behavior of the phantom.
\end{enumerate}

This architecture makes the relationships between geometry, material properties, numerical characterization, and control logic explicit and traceable throughout the system. Changes to the phantom specification—such as conductivity of materials—are reflected consistently in the numerical model and in the derived control mappings used by the software platform.

Based on the specifications and validation steps presented in this work, the physical phantom can be fabricated directly using the defined design and characterization pipeline, without requiring additional system-level definitions.
