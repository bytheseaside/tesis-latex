Electroencephalography (EEG) is a widely used, non-invasive technique for measuring electrical activity generated by neural populations in the brain.
Its applications span clinical diagnostics, neuroscience research, and the development of brain–computer interfaces (BCIs). As EEG systems evolve toward
higher channel counts, wearable form factors, and tighter integration with digital signal processing pipelines, the need for reliable and reproducible validation
methodologies becomes increasingly critical.

A fundamental challenge in EEG system evaluation lies in the absence of a known ground truth when measurements are performed on human subjects. Biological
variability, uncontrollable physiological states, and environmental noise make it difficult to isolate the performance of individual system components—such
as electrodes, acquisition hardware, or signal-processing algorithms—from the underlying neural activity. As a result, controlled testing and quantitative
benchmarking of EEG systems remain inherently limited when relying exclusively on in-vivo experiments.

Physical head phantoms provide a controlled alternative for EEG validation. By replicating the electrical behavior of the head and enabling the injection
of predefined signals, phantoms allow repeatable experiments under well-defined conditions. However, existing EEG phantom solutions exhibit several practical
limitations. Many rely on specialized or proprietary materials, involve complex or expensive manufacturing processes, or suffer from limited stability of their
electrical properties over time. These factors can hinder accessibility, long-term use, and adaptation across research contexts.

A further limitation of current approaches is the frequent separation between physical experimentation, numerical modeling, and signal-generation software.
Phantom heads are often treated as isolated test objects, without a computational framework that explicitly links injected signals, material properties, and measured
surface potentials. This separation complicates quantitative interpretation, limits systematic tuning, and reduces the usefulness of phantoms as long-term validation tools.

This thesis adopts a framework-based approach to EEG phantom development, focusing on modularity, reproducibility, and traceability across hardware, modeling,
and software domains. Rather than delivering a fixed implementation, the goal is to define a coherent workflow that can be adapted or extended, depending on
experimental requirements, available materials, or specific research goals. Each element of the system has been designed with the expectation that certain
parameters—such as geometry, material properties, or signal characteristics—may change, and that the effects of these changes should be both predictable and
manageable within the framework.

The complete system is structured around three main components:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Software platform}: A dedicated application enables the definition of target EEG-like signals and manages the corresponding signal
    routing to the internal electrode array. The software also supports visualization of system states and comparison between generated and measured
    signals, forming the operational interface between model outputs and hardware control.
    \item \textbf{Hardware specification and CAD model}: A parametric CAD model of the phantom is designed, allowing geometry, electrode placement,
    and internal routing to be easily reconfigured. A specific material and fabrication strategy is assumed in this work to anchor the design; however,
    the structure is explicitly defined to remain open to alternative choices. If another configuration better suits a different experimental context,
    the CAD model and associated parameters can be modified without breaking downstream compatibility.
    \item \textbf{Numerical modeling and signal mapping pipeline}: A finite element method (FEM) model is implemented to simulate the potential distribution
    within the phantom volume, based on its geometry and conductivity configuration. The raw simulation results are processed through a structured pipeline
    that extracts voltage-response relationships and translates them into control parameters directly usable by the software platform. If physical parameters
    such as geometry or conductivity are altered, the same pipeline can be applied to regenerate the required mappings, ensuring continuity without manual recalibration.
\end{enumerate}

This structured architecture allows the entire system to remain transparent and modifiable. Dependencies between components are explicitly modeled, so changes
introduced at any point in the chain—such as to the phantom's geometry or internal materials—can be propagated through the design, simulation, and control logic
in a consistent and traceable way. The result is a system that can be both reused as-is and efficiently adapted to new research needs, without the overhead of
re-deriving functional relationships or repeating foundational development steps.
