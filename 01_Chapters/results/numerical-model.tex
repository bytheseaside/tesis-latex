\subsubsection{Reconstruction Operator and Resolution Analysis}

Following the comparative analysis of candidate forward operators, the matrix $A_8$ was selected as the forward operator of the system for all subsequent analyses. 
This choice reflects a substantial improvement in numerical conditioning: the condition number was reduced from approximately $135$ (for $A_9$) to approximately $20$, at the modest cost of removing a single actuator (column). 
This trade-off was deemed favorable, as it significantly improves numerical stability and noise robustness while preserving sufficient actuation capability.

Since $A_8$ is full column rank and exhibits satisfactory numerical conditioning, the Moore--Penrose pseudoinverse was employed without regularization to obtain the reconstruction operator. 
In this setting, the associated resolution matrix
\[
R = A_8^{\dagger} A_8
\]
is theoretically equal to the identity matrix, reflecting the absence of a null space and the formal identifiability of all control variables.

For completeness, the resolution matrix was computed numerically. All off--diagonal entries were found to be on the order of $10^{-15}$ or smaller, consistent with floating--point round--off errors, confirming that
\[
R \approx I
\]
within numerical precision. As a result, the resolution matrix does not provide additional discriminatory information in this case.


\subsubsection{Effect of noise on reconstruction}
To assess the sensitivity of the reconstruction pipeline with respect to measurement noise magnitude, a set of numerical experiments was conducted using randomly generated excitation vectors. For each test case, a known excitation vector~$x$ was mapped to measurement space through the linear forward operator~$A$, and additive noise was introduced according to
\begin{equation}
    y_{\text{noisy}} = A x + \mu + \sigma Z,
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ denotes a deterministic noise offset, $\sigma$ the noise standard deviation, and $Z$ a standard Gaussian random variable.

The reconstructed excitation was obtained by applying the Moore--Penrose pseudoinverse of the forward operator to the noisy measurements,
\[
\hat{x} = A^{\dagger} y_{\text{noisy}}.
\]
For each test case, the reconstruction quality was quantified using the relative error
\[
\epsilon = \|x - \hat{x}\|_2 / \|x\|_2.
\]

Two families of excitation vectors were considered: \emph{dense} excitations, in which all degrees of freedom were active, and \emph{sparse} excitations, involving only one or two nonzero components.

    \label{fig:noise_case_errors}

Figure~\ref{fig:noise_case_errors} presents the reconstruction error for all 500 test cases (250 dense and 250 sparse) under a fixed noise configuration. Each curve corresponds to the relative reconstruction error of an individual test case, while dashed horizontal lines indicate the mean error for each excitation family.

The reconstruction error remains bounded across all test cases, with no isolated failures or extreme deviations. Sparse excitations consistently exhibit higher reconstruction error than dense excitations, reflecting their increased sensitivity to noise in the pseudoinverse-based reconstruction. Within each excitation family, the observed error values remain confined to a narrow range around the mean, indicating limited variability across randomly generated test cases.
