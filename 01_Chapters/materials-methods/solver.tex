Before solving Eq.~\eqref{eq:pde} using the finite element method, we rewrite it
in a weak (or variational) form. The idea of the weak form is simple: instead
of requiring the differential equation to hold at every point inside the domain
(which would demand very smooth solutions), we require it to hold only in an
average or integrated sense. This is achieved by multiplying the equation by a
smooth test function and integrating over the domain. After an integration by
parts step, the formulation only involves first derivatives, which is exactly
what finite element functions can represent~\cite{fem-org}.

With this idea in mind, let $\Omega$ denote the region of interest (the phantom
volume), $\partial\Omega$ its boundary, $\Gamma_D$ the parts of the boundary
where the potential is prescribed, and $\Gamma_N$ the parts where a flux
condition is applied. Let $v$ be an arbitrary test function that is null on
$\Gamma_D$. Multiplying the strong formulation of the problem by $v$ and
integrating over the domain gives
\[
\int_\Omega v\, \nabla\cdot(\sigma\nabla u)\, dx = 0.
\]
Applying integration by parts yields
\[
- \int_\Omega \sigma \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\, dx
+ \int_{\partial\Omega} v\, \sigma \nabla u \cdot n\, ds = 0.
\]
Because $v = 0$ on $\Gamma_D$, the boundary term reduces to the Neumann portion,
leading to the final weak formulation:
\[
\int_\Omega \sigma \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\, dx
= \int_{\Gamma_N} g\, v\, ds.
\]

The quantity $g$ on the right-hand side is simply the flux prescribed on
$\Gamma_N$, coming directly from the Neumann boundary condition
$\sigma\nabla u \cdot n = g$. This quantity specifies the normal current flux
through that part of the boundary. In our phantom, the outer
surface of the head is assumed to be electrically insulating
(see Section~\ref{subsubsec:insulation}), which corresponds to a homogeneous
Neumann condition; thus $g = 0$. Under this assumption, the right-hand side
vanishes and the weak problem reduces to
\[
\int_\Omega \sigma \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\, dx = 0
\qquad \forall\, v.
\]

\subsubsection{Solution sampling at probe locations}

After solving the forward problem, the computed voltage potential field must be sampled at specific
measurement locations to enable validation against experimental data or comparison with analytical
solutions. This sampling step is performed by a dedicated probing script that extracts potential
values at predefined coordinates on the phantom surface.

To establish consistent measurement locations across simulations and physical experiments, 21 probe positions
were defined following the international 10-20 EEG system (Fp1, Fp2, Fpz, F7, F3, Fz, F4, F8, T3, C3, Cz, C4,
T4, T5, P3, Pz, P4, T6, O1, Oz, O2). These standard electrode positions were located and marked directly on the
CAD geometry of the phantom model shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:head_shell_overview}, following the same procedure used
in clinical practice to position EEG electrodes on a patient's scalp. This ensures spatial correspondence between
simulated probe locations and physical measurement points on the fabricated phantom, covering frontal, central,
parietal, temporal, and occipital regions.

The probing tool takes three inputs: (1) the VTU solution file containing the scalar field $u$~(voltage potential),
(2)~a CSV file defining probe locations with their names and 3D coordinates $(x, y, z)$ extracted from the CAD
model, and (3)~an output path for the sampled results.

Since the probe coordinates may not coincide exactly with mesh vertices, the script first extracts the head shell
surface from the volume mesh (by thresholding \texttt{region\_id}), then uses a nearest-neighbor search to find the
closest surface point to each probe location. The voltage value at that point is recorded in the output CSV file
with columns: \texttt{name}, \texttt{x}, \texttt{y}, \texttt{z}, and \texttt{u\_volts}.

This approach ensures robust sampling even when probe coordinates are slightly displaced from the mesh surface due
to geometric tolerances or coordinate system differences. The resulting probe data enables quantitative comparison between
simulation cases and can be visualized directly in the interactive viewer for validation purposes.
