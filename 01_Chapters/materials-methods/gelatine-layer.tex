To validate the suitability of gelatine as a reliable material for the phantom design, the following laboratory experiments were designed. 
These experiments are intended as a qualitative material assessment, aimed at evaluating
tunability, batch-to-batch consistency, and behavioral regimes under varying salinity, rather
than as a precise metrological determination of tissue-equivalent conductivity values.

\subsubsection{Impedance Analysis and Material Sensitivity}\label{sec:gelatine_impedance_sensitivity}

The primary objective of this study is to measure the complex impedance of different gelatine solutions across a defined frequency spectrum.

\paragraph*{Prototype Fabrication}

The prototypes were constructed using a hydrogel composed of unflavored gelatine and deionized water. To analyze the correlation between salinity and impedance, distinct batches were prepared with varying concentrations of sodium chloride (NaCl).

The gelatine and salt were added to the deionized water at room temperature, keeping the gelatine at 30\% concentration and testing the NaCl at 0\%, 2.5\%, and 5\% concentrations. The solutions were heated to $60\,^\circ$C, thoroughly mixing to ensure homogeneity until the gelatine was fully dissolved, avoiding the formation of lumps and bubbles.

The mixtures were cast into standardized molds (derived from isometric soap molds) to ensure constant geometric dimensions across all samples. The samples were stored in a refrigeration unit at $2^\circ$C for a minimum duration of 12 hours. Once solidified, the prototypes were removed from the refrigerator and prepared for electrical testing at room temperature.

\paragraph*{Equipment and Setup}

The experimental setup utilizes the following instrumentation to generate signals and capture the electrical response of the phantom:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Signal generator: To provide sinusoidal input signals of varying frequencies and amplitudes.
    \item Two-channel oscilloscope: To simultaneously monitor the input signal ($V_{in}$) and the output signal ($V_{out}$) propagated through the medium.
    \item Custom electrodes: Insulated conductive wires with exposed tips inserted into the gelatine to serve as voltage injection or recording points as needed.
    \item Reference resistor ($R_{ref}$): A known resistor placed in series with the gelatine prototype to facilitate measurement.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph*{Frequency Response}

A sinusoidal signal was injected into the gelatine cubes arranged in a series circuit with the reference resistor. By measuring the voltage drop across the phantom and the phase shift relative to the source signal, the complex impedance ($Z$) and phase angle ($\theta$) were calculated for each NaCl concentration across a range of frequencies. The following metrics will be extracted from the measurements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Attenuation: The ratio of signal amplitude drop as it traverses the medium ($V_{out} / V_{in}$).
    \item Phase shift: The temporal delay ($\Delta \phi$) between the input and output waveforms.
\end{itemize}

By plotting these variables as a function of frequency, we determine the spectral behavior of the phantom material. Since the geometric shape factor remained constant, observed variations are attributed directly to the salt proportions.

\paragraph*{Concentration and Reproducibility}

To establish the reliability of the manufacturing process, two separate batches of the identical recipe were measured under identical conditions. This allows us to quantify the deviation between samples and establish the reproducibility of the protocol.

\paragraph*{Signal Sensitivity}

Two sources of signal injection were placed at fixed coordinates within the phantom as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:gelatine_prototype_1}.

The sources consisted of sinusoidal signals at $100 Hz$ for Input 1 and $900 Hz$ for Input 2, both of them with amplitudes of $500 mV$ peak-to-peak.

Potential measurements were taken at various surface locations of the phantom. By analyzing the recorded potentials, we assess the system's ability to differentiate between multiple simultaneous sources.
