\subsection{Phantom--Head Transfer Model}

Having validated the numerical solver and quantified discretization error using an analytical benchmark, the finite element model is now used to characterize the electrical behavior of the phantom--head geometry itself. In this context, the phantom is treated as a linear conductive system that maps prescribed internal excitations to measurable surface potentials.

The purpose of this section is to construct a linear transfer model for the phantom head, analyze its numerical properties, and define the procedure used to evaluate reconstruction performance under controlled conditions.

\subsubsection{Transfer Matrix Construction}

The phantom--head model comprises $N_e = 9$ internal electrodes, each capable of generating an electrical excitation. A set of basis stimulation patterns was defined by activating one electrode at a time, while all remaining electrodes were held at a fixed reference potential.

For each basis stimulus, the electrode under consideration was driven using a fixed potential configuration (\textit{tip} at $+1~\mathrm{V}$, \textit{ring} at $-1~\mathrm{V}$, \textit{sleeve} at $0~\mathrm{V}$), while all other electrode surfaces were explicitly assigned a Dirichlet potential of $0~\mathrm{V}$. All remaining boundaries followed the material and interface definitions of the phantom model. This procedure yields a well-defined and reproducible set of input excitations.

The outputs of the system were defined as the electric potentials evaluated at $N_m = 21$ measurement locations on the outer surface of the phantom, corresponding to the electrode positions of the international 10--20 EEG system.

For each basis excitation, a forward finite element simulation was performed and the resulting surface potentials were sampled at the measurement locations. The phantom--head transfer matrix $F \in \mathbb{R}^{N_m \times N_e}$ was then assembled by stacking the measured responses column-wise, such that the $i$-th column of $F$ represents the surface response to the $i$-th electrode excitation.

Under the assumption of linearity, the relationship between an arbitrary excitation vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^{N_e}$ and the resulting measurement vector $y \in \mathbb{R}^{N_m}$ can be written as
\[
y = F x.
\]

In this formulation, the excitation vector $x$ does not represent all electrode surface potentials independently. Instead, each component $x_i$ corresponds to the scalar excitation amplitude applied to the \emph{tip} of the $i$-th electrode. For each electrode, the remaining electrode surfaces are constrained by design: the ring potential is set to the opposite value of the tip, and the sleeve potential is fixed at zero. As a result, each electrode contributes a single degree of freedom to the system, and the vector $x$ fully parameterizes the applied boundary conditions used to construct the columns of $F$.


\subsubsection{Transfer Matrix Analysis}

The phantom--head transfer matrix was analyzed to characterize the numerical properties of the forward mapping between electrode excitations and surface measurements. The rank of $F$, its singular value spectrum, and its condition number were computed to assess identifiability and numerical stability of the system.

Column-wise similarity between electrode responses was evaluated using correlation- and coherence-based metrics in order to quantify linear dependencies and potential redundancy among excitation patterns. These analyses complement the singular value decomposition by providing pairwise measures of similarity between columns of the transfer matrix.

In addition to global matrix diagnostics, probe-wise statistics were computed to characterize the distribution of responses across measurement locations. For each probe, summary statistics including the mean response magnitude, standard deviation, and coefficient of variation were evaluated over the set of electrode excitations. These quantities provide a measure of the relative sensitivity and variability of each measurement location within the transfer model.

Several column-selection (electrode-reduction) strategies were evaluated as part of the transfer matrix analysis. The tested approaches included:
\begin{itemize}
    \item selection based on singular value contributions,
    \item correlation- and coherence-based redundancy criteria,
    \item condition-number–driven reduction strategies,
    \item energy-based metrics,
    \item residual- or reconstruction-error–based criteria.
\end{itemize}

For each strategy, reduced transfer matrices were constructed and then analyzed using the same numerical diagnostics as the full matrix. In addition, comparative analyses were performed to assess agreement and consistency across selection methods, including identification of electrodes consistently selected or discarded by multiple criteria.

\subsubsection{Reconstruction Operator Definition}

Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N_m \times N_d}$ denote a forward operator representing the phantom--head system, where $A$ may correspond to the full transfer matrix $F$ or to a reduced matrix obtained by selecting a subset of its columns. The relationship between excitation amplitudes $x \in \mathbb{R}^{N_d}$ and surface measurements $y \in \mathbb{R}^{N_m}$ is written as
\[
y = A x.
\]

A reconstruction operator $B$ was defined to estimate excitation amplitudes from measurements. Both the Moore--Penrose pseudoinverse and regularized pseudoinverse formulations were considered. When evaluating regularization, a zero-order Tikhonov formulation was used, with the regularization operator chosen as the identity matrix. Standard parameter-selection criteria, including the L-curve and generalized cross-validation (GCV) methods, were applied to assess the relevance of the regularization parameter $\lambda$.

The reconstructed excitation vector is obtained as
\[
\hat{x} = B y.
\]

\subsubsection{Resolution Matrix}

To assess identifiability and cross-talk independently of specific test cases, the resolution matrix
\[
R = B A
\]
was computed for the reconstruction operators under consideration. The resolution matrix maps true excitation amplitudes to their reconstructed counterparts and provides a compact description of how accurately and independently individual excitation channels can be recovered.

Quantitative diagnostics derived from $R$ included deviations of diagonal entries from unity, norms of off-diagonal contributions associated with cross-talk, and global measures quantifying the departure of $R$ from the identity matrix.

\subsubsection{Reconstruction Validation Tests}

Reconstruction performance was evaluated using a set of complementary validation tests designed to probe different aspects of the inverse mapping. These tests were formulated to separately assess algebraic invertibility, consistency with the finite element model, and the ability to reproduce physically realizable surface potential patterns.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Matrix-only reconstruction (algebraic consistency):}  
    Random excitation vectors $x_{\mathrm{true}}$ were generated and mapped to measurement space using the forward operator, $y = A x_{\mathrm{true}}$. The reconstructed excitation $\hat{x} = B y$ was then compared to $x_{\mathrm{true}}$. This test evaluates the intrinsic behavior of the inverse operator under idealized, noise-free conditions and isolates the effects of conditioning and cross-talk in excitation space.

    \item \textbf{FEM-consistent reconstruction:}  
    Random excitation vectors $x_{\mathrm{true}}$ were applied in the finite element solver to obtain measurement vectors $y_{\mathrm{FEM}}$. The reconstructed excitation $\hat{x} = B y_{\mathrm{FEM}}$ was compared to $x_{\mathrm{true}}$. This test assesses the consistency between the linear transfer model and the numerical forward solver, and captures the influence of discretization, sampling, and numerical approximation errors.

    \item \textbf{Feasible target tracking (round-trip validation):}  
    Physically realizable target patterns were defined as $y_{\mathrm{target}} = A x_{\mathrm{true}}$. The corresponding excitation $\hat{x} = B y_{\mathrm{target}}$ was computed and mapped forward to obtain $\hat{y} = A \hat{x}$. The comparison between $\hat{y}$ and $y_{\mathrm{target}}$ evaluates the ability of the inverse--forward pipeline to reproduce achievable surface potential patterns, independently of errors expressed in excitation space.

    \item \textbf{Noise and error sensitivity:}  
    Robustness to perturbations was evaluated by introducing noise at the measurement level, $y_{\eta} = y + \eta$. Both constant-amplitude and signal-dependent noise models were considered, informed by numerical error levels observed in the analytical benchmark. Reconstruction error was evaluated as a function of the noise level for the tests described above.
\end{enumerate}
