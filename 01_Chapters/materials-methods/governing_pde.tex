The numerical simulation of the phantom's electrical behavior is based on the interaction between the internal conductive medium and the 3D-printed shell. To transition from physical materials to a mathematical model, we verify that the phantom satisfies the four modeling assumptions established in the \textbf{Background} (Section~\ref{subsubsec:modeling_assumptions}).

\subsubsection{Physical Context and Dimensional Analysis}

The phantom has the dimensions of a realistic adult head, with a characteristic length scale of $L \approx  18-22~\text{cm}$. As detailed in Appendix~\ref{appendix:wavelength},  the electromagnetic wavelength $\lambda$ for the signals of interest (up to 100 Hz) in this case is on the order of several kilometers. 

Because the condition $L \ll \lambda$ is satisfied by several orders of magnitude, the system operates deep within the \textbf{Electroquasistatic Regime (Condition 1)}.


\subsubsection{Conductivity and Material Properties}
The phantom is modeled as a piecewise homogeneous domain $\Omega = \Omega_{\text{gel}} \cup \Omega_{\text{shell}}$

\begin{itemize}
    \item The inner medium ($\Omega_{\mathrm{gel}}$): The saline--gelatine mixture acts as the primary
    volume conductor. In the numerical model, the electrical conductivity of the inner layer is
    treated as a prescribed material property within the electroquasistatic framework, rather than
    as a quantity inferred from a specific experimental realization.

    The conductivity is specified such that the medium exhibits linear, isotropic, and Ohmic
    behavior over the EEG frequency range. This assumption is physically supported by the
    well-established tunability of gelatine-based media through ionic concentration, which enables
    operation within the desired conductive regime.

    Accordingly, a conductivity range of $\sigma_{\mathrm{gel}} \approx 0.3$--$0.4~\mathrm{S/m}$,
    consistent with reported values for human intracranial tissues, is adopted
    \cite{akor-2025}. Experimental studies further indicate that for frequencies below
    $100~\mathrm{Hz}$, $\sigma_{\mathrm{gel}}$ remains effectively frequency-independent, with
    negligible capacitive phase shifts \cite{geddes-1967,Chandra2000}.
    \item \textbf{The conductive shell  ($\Omega_{\text{shell}}$):}  The outer boundary consists of a 3D-printed conductive PLA shell. Manufacturer data \textbackslash{}cite\{cpla-datasheet\} and recent studies \textbackslash{}cite\{akor-2025, tilve-martinez-2024\} indicate that 3D-printed polymers exhibit orthotropic conductivity due to the layer-by-layer deposition process. While resistivities vary between the $X-Y$ plane ($19.2~\Omega\cdot\text{cm}$) and the $Z$-axis ($33.6~\Omega\cdot\text{cm}$), research suggests that anisotropy can be mitigated through optimized printing parameters such as extrusion temperature and path planning~\cite{paz-2020, nowka-2024}. \\
    For this model, a representative scalar value of $\sigma_{shell} = 4.1~\text{S/m}$ is defined. This value serves as a balanced estimate for a reasonably optimized print, though $\sigma_{\text{shell}}$ remains a user-definable parameter to allow for sensitivity analysis or material-specific characterization.
\end{itemize}

Given these properties, \textbf{ Condition 2 (Purely Resistive Media) }is satisfied, as the high conductivity $\sigma$ of both regions ensures that conduction currents dominate over displacement currents ($\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon$). Furthermore, the verified frequency-independence and Ohmic behavior confirm that \textbf{Condition 3 (Linear and Instantaneous Response) }holds.

\subsubsection{Boundary Conditions and Interface.}
To solve for the potential $u$, we must define how the phantom interacts with its environment:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{External Insulation (Neumann BC): } The phantom is a closed volume surrounded by air. Since air is a near-perfect insulator, no current flows out of the shell surface. This is represented by a homogeneous Neumann boundary condition:
    $$ \sigma \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega_{\text{ext}} $$
    
    \item \textbf{High-Impedance Electrodes: }Recording electrodes on the shell surface are connected to amplifiers with input impedances $>100~\text{M}\Omega$. Consequently, the current they draw is negligible, allowing us to treat these locations as passive observation points where the potential $u$ \textit{floats} without perturbing the field.

    \item \textbf{Internal Interface: }A \textit{watertight} conformal interface between the gel and the shell is expected. This implies interface continuity, where both the potential and the normal current density are continuous across the internal boundary ($\Omega_{\text{gel}} \cap \Omega_{\text{shell}}$).
\end{itemize}

The absence of internal current sinks and the perfectly insulated exterior boundary ensure that the divergence of the current density is zero throughout the volume, satisfying \textbf{Condition 4 (Conservation of Current)}.


\subsubsection{PDE Formulation and Simulation Cases}
Because the four conditions mentioned in the Background are satisfied by the physical parameters and the environment of our phantom, the electric potential $u(\mathbf{x}, t)$ can be described by the variable-coefficient Laplace equation:

\begin{equation}
-\nabla \cdot \big(\sigma(\mathbf{x})\, \nabla u(\mathbf{x},t)\big) = 0, \qquad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in \Omega_{\text{gel}} \cup \Omega_{\text{shell}}.
\label{eq:pde_final}
\end{equation}

The external shell surface was modeled with a homogeneous Neumann boundary 
condition ($\sigma \partial u/\partial n = 0$) as mentioned previously, 
representing the insulating air boundary. This equation was solved numerically 
using the finite element method (FEniCSx) for multiple electrode configurations. 
Base simulations employed symmetrical Jack stimulation with $V_{i,T} = -V_{i,R}$ 
and $V_{i,S} = 0$ for all nine electrodes ($i = 1,\ldots,9$), representing typical 
differential voltage injection. Additional cases with randomized electrode voltages 
were also simulated to explore the full parameter space of potential distributions.
