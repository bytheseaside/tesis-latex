The development of reliable electroencephalography (EEG) sensing systems requires rigorous validation methods to ensure accurate signal acquisition~\cite{akor-2025}. Phantom heads serve as essential testing platforms that offer consistent electrical characteristics suitable for systematic evaluation of electrode performance without the intrinsic unknowns of on-person experiments~\cite{Owda2020}.

These phantoms allow a pre-recorded electrophysiology signal to be played out, which can then be collected and analyzed by the novel electrode or instrumentation system, or neurotechnological application to be tested~\cite{Owda2020}.

\subsection{Design Types of Phantom Heads}\label{subsec:phantom_designs}

\subsubsection{Single Layer (Homogeneous) Phantoms}

Single layer phantoms are the most widely used~\cite{Owda2020}. They are typically constructed from a single material that aims to replicate the average electrical properties of the human head~\cite{9475461}. These phantoms are simpler to fabricate and are often used for preliminary testing of EEG electrodes and systems~\cite{Collier2012}.

\subsubsection{Multi-Layer Phantoms}

Multi-layer phantoms typically consist of three layers representing the scalp, skull, and brain~\cite{9475461}. They are frequently used in combination with materials like saline gels or hydrogels and conductive polymers/carbon-filled silicones to create these layered models~\cite{Owda2020}. This design enhances the anatomical and electrical realism of the phantom, allowing for more accurate simulation of signal propagation through different tissue types~\cite{Collier2012}. Multi-layer phantoms are particularly valuable for evaluating electrode performance in conditions that closely mimic real-world scenarios~\cite{akor-2025}.

\subsubsection{Real-Tissue Phantoms}

Real-tissue phantoms incorporate actual biological tissue, typically from animals or cadavers, into the model~\cite{Owda2020, 9475461}. These are principally used for advanced research where more realistic anatomic representations are required. For example, human head skull phantoms filled with gelatine have been used to investigate the effect of electrode localization on EEG and magnetoencephalogram (MEG)~\cite{9475461}.

Although these phantoms can give similar frequency response characteristics to gelatine phantoms, gelatine phantoms are often selected as the preferred approach because they are more manageable, can be generated in the lab at any time, and provide more control over the electrical properties~\cite{9475461}.

\subsubsection{Advanced Design and Fabrication Trends}

Currently, hybrid methods are gaining popularity~\cite{9475461}. These methods combine synthetic and biological materials to balance fidelity and practicality. Furthermore, the use of advanced technologies such as 3D printing and the functionalization of materials with nanocomposites is revolutionizing the design of phantom heads~\cite{akor-2025}.

\subsection{Materials}\label{subsec:phantom_materials}

Phantom heads are typically fabricated using biocompatible and conductive materials that mimic the dielectric properties of human tissue~\cite{Collier2012}. These materials include carbon-filled silicones and conductive polymers, such as saline gels or hydrogels~\cite{Owda2020}. These phantom devices aim to be a good electrical equivalent of the human body and its tissues~\cite{9475461}.

The choice of materials directly influences the model's capacity to reproduce the propagation of electrical signals, which in turn affects the reliability of data collection using EEG electrodes~\cite{akor-2025}.

The greatest challenge in creating these validation platforms is balancing anatomical accuracy with the practicality of the materials. This must ensure both the durability and stability of the materials over time, as well as their biosimilitude or representativeness. Achieving this balance is crucial because high-performance phantoms, such as commercial injection-molded or multi-material models~\cite{akor-2025} are often expensive and complex, whereas simpler, accessible solutions like saline or hydrogels lack durability and consistency over time~\cite{9475461}.

\subsubsection{Saline Gels and Hydrogels}

Saline gels and hydrogels are fundamental materials in the creation of phantom heads due to their capacity to replicate the electrical and mechanical properties of biological tissues~\cite{Owda2020}. In particular, gelatine-based phantoms are widely used to mimic the electrical properties of the human body. The electrical properties of these phantoms can be tuned by adjusting the concentrations of gelatine and sodium chloride (NaCl) added to the mixture~\cite{9475461}.

Saline gels are typically composed of a gelatinous matrix, such as gelatine or agar/agarose, mixed with saline solutions that commonly include sodium chloride to adjust the ionic conductivity, as NaCl provides ions for conduction~\cite{9475461}. Different levels of NaCl allow the electrical properties to be tuned to match various tissues of interest~\cite{Marchal01011989}.\nota{Añadir valores relevantes}

However, these materials present limitations in their stability, such as evaporation~\cite{Owda2020} and degradation~\cite{Tseghai2021ALT}, which affects their use in prolonged tests. The resulting increase in contact impedance magnitude over time is attributed to the water content evaporating, which makes the phantom drier~\cite{C5TC01888A}. The conductivity of the material also presents changes based on temperature, requiring storage at a constant low temperature (e.g., 2.3°C)~\cite{9475461}. Gelatin-based phantoms generally show durability in their electrical response over the course of seven days under ideal conditions~\cite{9475461}.

Hydrogels are gel materials like gelatine and agarose used in phantoms~\cite{Owda2020}. Those composed of cross-linked hydrophilic polymers stand out for their capacity to absorb large amounts of water and maintain stable electrical conductivity~\cite{C5TC01888A}. Tested ion gels achieved conductivity values in the range of 0.1 $\text{S/m}$ to 1 $\text{S/m}$ at relevant temperatures~\cite{C5TC01888A}. This conductivity range is appropriate for mimicking tissues like the brain (0.12 to 0.48 $\text{S/m}$) and the scalp (0.43 $\text{S/m}$)~\cite{Owda2020}. The three-dimensional structure of these materials allows for the customization of both electrical and mechanical properties~\cite{Uhrhan2024}. For example, materials can be functionalized by incorporating conductive additives such as carbon nanofibers~\cite{Slipher2018} or multi-walled carbon nanotubes~\cite{MAGSOOD2021111705}.

At relevant frequencies (in electrophysiology, up to 1000 Hz~\cite{9475461}), saline solutions maintain conductivity stability, with a variation under 2\% between 0.1 Hz and 1 kHz~\cite{Chandra2000}. Hydrogels are designed to offer structural stability under physiological conditions; for instance, cholinium ion gels demonstrated signal stability over 72 hours in cutaneous recordings~\cite{C5TC01888A}. Recent advances utilize ionic liquids in ion gels because they have negligible vapor pressure and evaporation rate, which helps overcome the stability limitations associated with traditional aqueous electrolytes~\cite{C5TC01888A}.

Both saline gels and hydrogels are frequently used in combination to create multi-layer models that simulate both the rigidity of the skull~\cite{Collier2012} and the conductive properties of internal tissues~\cite{Owda2020}. This practice significantly advances the precision of phantom heads for biomedical studies and medical device calibration~\cite{Collier2012}.

\subsubsection{Carbon-Filled Silicones and Conductive Polymers}

Carbon-filled silicones and conductive polymers have gained popularity in the development of EEG studies~\cite{Collier2012}.

Carbon-filled silicones (elastomers) combine the intrinsic flexibility of silicone with adjustable conductivity provided by carbon particles, such as in carbon nanofibers (CNFs)~\cite{Slipher2018} or carbon black~\cite{akor-2025}. These materials are composite elastomers, often polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) filled with CNFs, and are used to achieve controlled electrical properties~\cite{akor-2025}. This type of material is ideal for mimicking the electrical and mechanical properties of biological tissues~\cite{Collier2012}. Specifically, modified silicone and urethane materials allow the accurate reproduction of the conductivities required for phantom layers~\cite{Collier2012}.

Furthermore, they offer excellent durability and can adapt to complex geometries~\cite{Owda2020, akor-2025}. This adaptability is achieved through fabrication techniques like molding/casting or 3D printing, where conductive polymer composites using specialized carbon-loaded thermoplastics are utilized to create structured conductive models~\cite{akor-2025}.

On the other hand, conductive polymers stand out for their adjustable intrinsic conductivity~\cite{C5TC01888A}. The electrical properties of polymer systems can be customized by incorporating conductive additives such as carbon nanofibers~\cite{9475461}. These polymers are especially useful for replicating the electrical properties of more conductive tissues, such as gray matter or cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), the latter of which has a known conductivity of 1.79 $\text{S/m}$~\cite{Collier2012}. Certain polymer systems, such as cholinium-based ion gels, are known as bioionic liquids due to their biocompatibility, biodegradability, and low toxicity, which makes them attractive for long-term cutaneous electrophysiology applications~\cite{C5TC01888A}.

The combination of these materials allows for the creation of sophisticated multi-layer models. In these phantoms, silicone (often with conductive fillers) may be used to simulate more rigid tissues, such as the skull, while conductive polymer composites replicate softer, more conductive regions, such as the brain~\cite{Collier2012, akor-2025}.

However, conductive polymers and related polymer systems face challenges such as degradation in humid conditions~\cite{paz-2020}. Additionally, the fabrication of sophisticated multi-material phantoms using these advanced composites often involves complex multi-step processes and elevated costs~\cite{akor-2025}.

\subsection{Signal Generation}

The generation of electrical signals within a phantom head is the core mechanism enabling the controlled validation of new electrode and instrumentation designs~\cite{9475461}. Unlike experiments conducted on human subjects where the recorded signal is intrinsically unknown, phantoms allow a pre-recorded electrophysiology signal or a synthetically created signal to be played out, providing a known signal for the test system to collect and compare against~\cite{Owda2020}. This is usually achieved by injecting precisely controlled currents or voltages into the phantom's conductive medium via a set of internal drive electrodes~\cite{akor-2025}.

Phantom designs then consist of primarily three components: the electrode array that injects the signal into the phantom, the electrode driver that allows the independent control of each electrode, and the phantom head itself that contains said electrode array~\cite{LEAHY1998159}.

As phantom head design and composition where already covered in Subsection~\ref{subsec:phantom_designs} and Subsection~\ref{subsec:phantom_materials} respectively, this section focuses on the signal generation module (electrode array and driver) and the key characteristics of the generated signals.

\subsubsection{Signal Requirements}

Phantom heads are designed to mimic the frequency spectrum of human brain activity, which is characterized by a wide range of frequencies. Specific frequency bands used in phantom validation include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Standard bands: Phantoms are often tested against standard clinical bands, such as Delta (0.5{-}4 Hz), Theta (4{-}7 Hz), Alpha (8{-}12 Hz) and Beta (13{-}30 Hz)~\cite{akor-2025}.
    \item Higher frequencies: Gamma, 30{-}80 Hz and higher, are more difficult to study in the human body as they are heavily attenuated by the skull and scalp layers, and obfuscated by muscular and ocular artifacts~\cite{FREEMAN20031053}. Head phantom validation often involves ensuring that the components accurately mimic these behaviours~\cite{9475461}.
\end{itemize}

The amplitude of the EEG under physiological conditions ranges from 15 to 150 \( \mu\text{V} \)~\cite{Naydenov2022}. However, the internal generators (electrodes) must drive significantly higher voltages to account for the attenuation caused by volume conduction through the phantom material~\cite{LEAHY1998159}. The electronics must provide a wide dynamic range, capable of generating signals that result in low surface potentials to reduce noise~\cite{Morsalin2020}.

\subsubsection{Electrode Driver}

The electrode driver acts as the interface between the digital simulation software (usually on a PC) and the physical phantom, generating complex, arbitrary waveforms used to mimic neural activity~\cite{LEAHY1998159}.

Controlling the driver means knowing the exact location, orientation, amplitude, and timing of the source: the ``ground truth'' data~\cite{LEAHY1998159}. This known signal is crucial for validating the performance of EEG electrodes and acquisition systems, as it allows for objective comparisons between the generated signal and the recorded data.~\cite{Audette2020}

\subsubsection{Electrode Construction}
The driver controls the known electrical signals that are injected into physical electrodes embedded in the phantom head. These electrodes can be constructed in various ways depending on the design requirements of the phantom:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The most common are dipoles constructed from semi-rigid coaxial cables, where the inner conductor and the outer shield are exposed at the tip (with a gap of roughly 0.5 mm to 2 mm) to form the two poles of the dipole (see Figure~\ref{fig:dipole_leahy}). This design is favored because the semi-rigid nature of the cable allows for precise positioning and bending to specific orientations within the phantom volume~\cite{LEAHY1998159}.
    \item For high-fidelity phantoms, platinum electrodes are used as they are chemically inert and resistant to corrosion, which is a major concern in saline environments~\cite{Machts2019}.
    \item For lower-cost phantoms, standard 3.5 mm TRS audio jacks are used. While less electrochemically stable than platinum, they are readily available and provide a standardized connection interface.~\cite{Downey2023}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Electronic module architecture}

The choice of architecture dictates the fidelity, bandwidth, and flexibility of the phantom. Some common architectures include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Computer audio interface: frequently used for this task due to their high resolution (typically 24-bit), excellent signal-to-noise ratio (>100 dB), and low cost~\cite{Collier2012}. The downside is that they tether the phantom to a computer, introducing ground loops and mains noise unless USB isolators are used~\cite{LEAHY1998159}.
    \item Microcontroller-based architecture: for portable, battery-powered phantoms. A usual choice is the Teensy 3.2 that features a built-in 12-bit digital-to-analog converter (DAC)~\cite{PJRC_Teensy}. While having lower resolution than audio interfaces, 12 bits provides sufficient dynamic range for many verification tasks, especially when the signal is generated at a higher amplitude and then attenuated analogically~\cite{LEAHY1998159}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Signal Acquisition and Analysis}

Data is typically collected using digital EEG amplifiers with sampling frequencies ranging from 250 Hz to 5 kHz~\cite{cita}. To prevent aliasing and remove noise, signal filters are applied prior to sampling, such as a 0.1 Hz high-pass and 100 Hz low-pass filters~\cite{LEAHY1998159}.

\subsubsection{The 10{-}20 Electrode System}

The 10{-}20 electrode system is an international standard for placing scalp electrodes during electroencephalography (EEG) and event-related potential (ERP) studies~\cite{klem-1999}. It serves as a foundational grid for ensuring standardized and reproducible measurements across different subjects and laboratories.

A standard 10{-}20 array typically consists of 21 electrodes. Every position is identified by a letter and a number. The letters correspond to the brain regions they cover: 

\begin{itemize}
    \item F (frontal),
    \item T (temporal),
    \item C (central),
    \item P (parietal),
    \item O (occipital). 
\end{itemize}

The numbers indicate the hemisphere: 

\begin{itemize}
    \item Odd numbers for the left hemisphere,
    \item Even numbers for the right hemisphere,
    \item Z for midline positions.
\end{itemize}
