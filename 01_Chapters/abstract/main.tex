\begin{abstract}
The development and calibration of Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) and electroencephalography (EEG) analysis algorithms require reliable, ground-truth signal sources. Commercial EEG simulators are often expensive or closed-source, limiting their accessibility for academic research. This thesis presents the design, implementation, and modular validation of a cost-effective, hybrid EEG phantom head capable of generating reproducible synthetic brain signals.

The proposed system comprises two integrated components: a physical head model and a dedicated software control platform. The physical model is designed as a bilayer structure consisting of a conductive PLA cranial shell and a saline gelatine volume, with TRS audio jacks placed as dipoles on the inner surface. Experimental characterization of the inner medium demonstrated that the electrical impedance of the gelatine is both tunable via ionic concentration and reproducible. A Finite Element Method (FEM) numerical model was developed to validate the electromagnetic behavior of this architecture, confirming the feasibility of mapping internal excitations to measurable surface potentials and establishing the stability of an 8-channel scheme. An electronic signal conditioning module was designed to amplify and drive the audio signals into the phantom head, ensuring safe and accurate transduction.

Parallel to the hardware design, a cross-platform desktop application was developed using an asynchronous architecture (React, Electron, and Python). The software provides deterministic 8-channel signal generation with real-time visualization and integration with OpenBCI acquisition hardware.

Due to manufacturing constraints, the system evaluation was conducted through a modular verification framework. The results confirm the quality of the generated signals, the validity of the numerical forward operator, and the robust reproducibility of the phantom materials. Collectively, these findings validate the proposed architecture as a viable, open, and modular instrument for EEG simulation.

\end{abstract}
