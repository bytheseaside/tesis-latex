This work presented the design, implementation, and modular validation of a comprehensive \gls{eeg} phantom-head platform. While financial constraints necessitated a shift from full physical integration to a model-based verification strategy, the results demonstrate that the proposed architecture satisfies the fundamental requirements for a cost-effective, reproducible, and adaptable research tool.

The proposed solution relies on a modular architecture where the phantom head numerical model, hardware and software platform are treated as interconnected but independently verifiable components.

The phantom design adopts an anthropomorphic geometry constructed from accessible materials. Experimental characterization confirmed that the gelatine-based conductive medium yields reproducible impedance behavior suitable for transmitting \gls{eeg}-like signals. The combination of these material properties with the anatomical geometry allows for the effective propagation of spatially distributed surface potentials, enabling meaningful interaction between internal excitation and surface-level measurement.

Rather than serving merely as a post-hoc analysis, the numerical characterization functions as an integral part of the system architecture, explicitly linking simulation results to practical implementation. From informing hardware design choices, to providing the theoretical foundation for future calibration workflows.

The development of the software environment resulted in a desktop application capable of deterministic signal generation and real-time data acquisition. The adoption of a modern asynchronous architecture ensures responsive performance and seamless integration with OpenBCI hardware, successfully closing the loop between signal generation and acquisition.

Finally, the validation of the individual subsystems confirms that the hardware and software components operate as intended, while the numerical analysis validates the theoretical and physical feasibility of the design. This component-level verification—backed by a model that confirms the system definition is physically sound—\textbf{supports the expectation that the full physical realization will be successful and meet the design specifications.}
